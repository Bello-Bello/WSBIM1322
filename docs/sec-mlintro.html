Chapter 6 Introduction to statistical learning

The next chapters will focus on statistical (hypothesis testing in chapter 7) and general machine learning (chapters 8, 9 and 10). Before diving into the technical details, it is useful to learn (or remind outselves) why these techniques are so incredibly important when considering generating and analysing high throughput biomedical data. Indeed, these tools will not only help in extracting meaningful results from a data, but (1) understanding what they do, how to apply them, and their respective limitations, allows to frame the questions more accurately, and (2) to define what data is needed before generating it.

As before, imagine that instead of 20 correlations, we would need to consider thousands thereof!

In additionnal complication with large data is the appearance of spurious positive results, called false positives. In the example below, we calculate Pearon correlation coefficients between two vectors\(x\) and\(y\):

\[r_{xy} = \frac{\sum_{i = 1}^{n}(x_{i} - \bar x)(y_{i} - \bar y)}{\sqrt{\sum_{i = 1}^{n}(x_{i} - \bar x)^{2}} \sqrt{\sum_{i = 1}^{n}(y_{i} - \bar y)^{2}}}\]

where \(n\) is the length of the vector, \(x_{i}\) and \(y_{i}\) are the \(i_{th}\) elements of the \(x\) and y$ vectors, \(\bar x\) and \(\bar y\) are the respective means.

A correlation coefficient ranges between -1 (for anti-correlated vectors) to 1 (for perfectly correlated vectors). Non-correlated data have a correlation coefficient close to 0.

Figure 6.3: Example of correlation, anti-correlation and lack thereof.

► Question

Generate two random vectors x and y of length 60 using rnorm. Plot them, add a linear regression line and calculate their correlation coefficient using the cor function.

► Solution 

x <- rnorm(60)
y <- rnorm(60)

## with base graphics
plot(x, y)
abline(lm(y ~ x))

cor(x, y)
## [1] 0.08567754
## or with ggplot2
ggplot(data.frame(x, y),
       aes(x, y)) +
    geom_point() + geom_smooth(method = "lm")

Let’s expand the code above and calculate the correlation coefficient between hundreds of random vectors and choose the choose the best one (in abolute terms) among those. Let’s repeat this procedure 1000 times to obtain 1000 best correlations (based on Fan, Han, and Liu (2014Fan, Jianqing, Fang Han, and Han Liu. 2014. “Challenges of Big Data analysis.” National Science Review 1 (2): 293–314. https://doi.org/10.1093/nsr/nwt032.)). The figure below shows the distribution of these 1000 best correlations when comparing 800 or 6400 vectors. We see that with more (random) data, we see that the risk of obtaining spurious correlations if substantially increased.

► Question

Calculate 100 correlation coefficients for pairs of random vectors or length 60. What are the mean, maximum and minumum of your simulated coefficients? Out of those 100 coefficients, how many would you consider of interest if you didn’t know they were produced by random data?

► Solution 

set.seed(42)
rs <- replicate(100, cor(rnorm(10), rnorm(10)))
summary(rs)
##       Min.    1st Qu.     Median       Mean    3rd Qu.       Max. 
## -0.8194367 -0.2280550 -0.0009475  0.0055000  0.2118503  0.7977295
## Assuming a totally arbitrary value of 0.75, we would get
rs[abs(rs) > .75]
## [1]  0.7941876  0.7977295 -0.8194367
hist(rs)
rug(rs)
abline(v = rs[abs(rs) > .75], col = "red", lty = "dotted")
